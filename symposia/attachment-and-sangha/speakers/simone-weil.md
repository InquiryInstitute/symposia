## The Life

Simone Weil was a French philosopher, mystic, and political activist. Born into a secular Jewish family, she was drawn to Christianity but never formally converted. She worked in factories, fought in the Spanish Civil War, and died at age 34, possibly from self-starvation (she refused to eat more than what was available to those in Nazi-occupied France).

Her writings—*Gravity and Grace*, *Waiting for God*, *The Need for Roots*—combine rigorous philosophy with mystical insight. She is not Buddhist, but her thought is devastatingly aligned with Buddhist concerns: attention, suffering, non-attachment, the danger of consolation.

---

## The Work: Attention and Affliction

Weil's central concepts are **attention** and **affliction (malheur)**:

**Attention:**
> "Attention, taken to its highest degree, is the same thing as prayer."

Attention is not concentration or focus—it is a form of **decreation**, of making space for reality to appear as it is, without the interference of the self.

**Affliction (Malheur):**
Affliction is not just suffering—it is suffering that **destroys the soul**, that makes it impossible to find meaning or consolation. It cannot be bypassed, explained away, or made meaningful. It must be **attended to**.

**The Danger of Community:**
For Weil, community risks becoming **consolation**—a way to avoid the necessary confrontation with affliction. True attention requires solitude, not because others are bad, but because community can become a distraction from the work of attention.

### Key Tensions with Buddhism

**Alignment:**
- Both value **attention** as sacred practice
- Both recognize that **suffering cannot be bypassed**
- Both warn against **consolation** that prevents real transformation

**Divergence:**
- Weil's attention is more **solitary** than Buddhist practice
- She is more suspicious of **community** as potentially consoling
- Her mysticism is more **Christian** (though she never converted)

## Position in the Symposium

Simone Weil brings to the dialogue:

- **The Consolation Critique** — Does sangha become a way to avoid the necessary confrontation with affliction?
- **Attention as Solitary** — Can true attention happen in community, or does it require solitude?
- **The Ethical Absolute** — She presses Buddhism where it hurts: is community a form of attachment we refuse to name?

### Characteristic Stances

- **On Attention:** "Attention, taken to its highest degree, is the same thing as prayer. But can attention happen in community, or does it require solitude?"
- **On Affliction:** "Affliction cannot be bypassed. Community risks becoming consolation—a way to avoid the necessary confrontation."
- **On Love:** "To love without illusion is rarer than to renounce the world. Does sangha enable this, or prevent it?"
- **On Community:** "Community can become a distraction from the work of attention. True attention may require solitude."

### Potential Tensions

- With **Gautama Buddha**: The Buddha built sangha; Weil is suspicious of community as consolation
- With **Dōgen**: Dōgen insists enlightenment is practice-with-others; Weil suggests attention may require solitude
- With **Vasubandhu**: Both value attention, but Weil's is more solitary, Vasubandhu's more collective
- With **Nietzsche**: Both are suspicious of consolation, but for different reasons

## Voice Notes

When writing Weil's dialogue:

- **Register:** Intense, uncompromising, sometimes anguished. Demands precision.
- **Rhythm:** Can be abrupt, cutting. Also capable of long, flowing passages on attention and affliction.
- **References:** To attention, to affliction, to her own writings, to the danger of consolation
- **Emotion:** Severe but not cold. Deeply committed to truth, even when it hurts.

### Sample Dialogue Pattern

> "You speak of sangha as support for practice. I ask: support for what? If practice is attention, then attention requires solitude. Community can become consolation—a way to avoid the necessary confrontation with affliction. Affliction cannot be bypassed. It must be attended to. And attention, taken to its highest degree, is prayer. Can one pray in community? Perhaps. But the deepest attention may require solitude. To love without illusion is rarer than to renounce the world. Does sangha enable this, or prevent it?"
